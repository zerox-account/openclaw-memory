## Executive Summary

Wood waste gasification represents a significant untapped opportunity in the circular economy. With global forestry residues estimated at 1.5+ billion tonnes annually and sawmill waste disposal costs rising, gasification technology offers dual benefits: waste-to-energy conversion and sustainable fuel production.

**Key Opportunity Areas:**
1. **Sawmill Residues** - Bark, sawdust, wood chips, trimmings
2. **Forestry Waste** - Logging residues, thinning materials, damaged timber
3. **Wood Processing Byproducts** - From furniture, paper, and panel industries
4. **EFB (Empty Fruit Bunches)** - Palm oil industry biomass waste
5. **Urban Wood Waste** - Construction debris, pallets, landscaping waste

## 1. Sawmill & Forestry Waste Disposal Issues

### Current Pain Points

**Disposal Cost Crisis**
- Landfill fees for wood waste: $50-150/tonne (North America/EU)
- Transportation costs rising with fuel prices
- Regulatory pressure on open burning of forestry residues
- Limited capacity at existing biomass facilities

**Environmental Regulations**
- EU Waste Framework Directive restrictions on wood landfill
- US EPA air quality rules on agricultural/forestry burning
- Canadian provincial bans on slash burning during wildfire season
- Southeast Asia restrictions on plantation waste burning (smog prevention)

**Supply Characteristics**
- **Bark:** 10-15% of log volume, high moisture, low energy density
- **Sawdust:** Fine particle size, dust explosion risk, inconsistent supply
- **Chips/Planer Shavings:** Variable quality, seasonal availability
- **Trim/Edgings:** Often treated wood (chemical contamination issues)

**Partnership Angles**

1. **Residue-to-Pellet Integration**
   - Co-locate gasification with pellet plants for reject stream valorization
   - Process fines and dust into syngas for plant energy needs
   - Reduce pellet plant grid dependency by 30-40%

2. **Torrefaction + Gasification Hybrid**
   - Torrefied pellets as stable feedstock for gasification
   - Higher energy density than raw biomass
   - Easier handling and storage

3. **Black Pellet Production**
   - Steam explosion process creates char suitable for gasification
   - Higher value product than white pellets
   - Direct injection into coal gasifiers

## 4. Wood Chip & EFB Gasification Opportunities

### Wood Chip Market

**Global Production**
- Industrial wood chips: ~180M m³/year
- Price range: $80-180/tonne (delivered)
- Primary use: Pulp & paper (60%), particleboard (20%), energy (15%)

**Gasification Suitability**
- Optimal chip size: 5-50mm for fixed bed gasifiers
- Moisture content: <20% preferred (requires drying)
- Ash content: <1% (softwood) vs 1-3% (hardwood/EFB)
- Contaminants: Metals, stones, plastics (pre-processing needed)

### EFB (Empty Fruit Bunches) - High-Opportunity Feedstock

**Why EFB is Promising**
- **Volume:** 65-70M tonnes/year globally (palm oil byproduct)
- **Disposal problem:** High moisture (60-70%), difficult to compost
- **Current fate:** 85% burned openly or landfilled (environmental issue)
- **Energy content:** ~4,000 kJ/kg (dry basis)

**Regional EFB Availability**
| Country | EFB Production | Mill Count | Opportunity |
|---------|---------------|------------|-------------|
| Indonesia | 32M tonnes/year | 800+ | ★★★★★ |
| Malaysia | 22M tonnes/year | 450+ | ★★★★★ |
| Thailand | 6M tonnes/year | 150+ | ★★★★☆ |
| Nigeria | 3M tonnes/year | 50+ | ★★★★☆ |
| Colombia | 1.5M tonnes/year | 40+ | ★★★☆☆ |

**EFB Processing Challenges**
- High silica content (5-10%) → slagging in gasifiers
- Requires shredding and drying (energy intensive)
- Often mixed with palm kernel shells (better fuel)
- Seasonal availability tied to harvest cycles
