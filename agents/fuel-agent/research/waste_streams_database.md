# Global Waste Streams Database
## Underutilized High-Cost Waste Streams for Energy Recovery

**Research Date:** March 2026  
**Sources:** World Bank, FAO, EPA, Academic Literature

---

## Executive Summary

This database identifies underutilized high-cost waste streams globally that present opportunities for waste-to-energy (WTE) conversion. The analysis covers municipal solid waste, agricultural residues, industrial waste, food processing waste, and forestry residues.

**Key Findings:**
- Global waste generation: 2.01 billion tonnes annually (2016), projected to reach 3.40 billion tonnes by 2050
- At least 33% of waste is mismanaged globally through open dumping or burning
- High disposal costs create economic incentives for alternative utilization
- Significant energy potential remains untapped across all waste categories

## 7. ECONOMIC ANALYSIS

### Disposal Cost vs. Energy Value

| Waste Type | Disposal Cost ($/tonne) | Energy Value* ($/tonne) | Net Benefit ($/tonne) |
|------------|------------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Mixed MSW | $40-200 | $30-50 | ($10)-(-150) |
| Agricultural residues | $10-40 | $40-60 | $0-$50 |
| Industrial organic | $100-500 | $60-150 | ($40)-(-350) |
| Food waste | $40-150 | $35-55 | ($5)-(-95) |
| Wood waste | $30-150 | $45-70 | ($5)-(-80) |
| Plastic waste | $100-400 | $100-180 | ($0)-(-220) |

*Energy value at $50/MWh

### Break-Even Analysis

Waste-to-energy becomes economically viable when:
1. Disposal costs exceed $60-80/tonne
2. Energy prices exceed $60-80/MWh
3. Gate fees + energy revenue > operating costs
4. Scale exceeds 50,000-100,000 tonnes/year

## 10. CONCLUSIONS & RECOMMENDATIONS

### Key Opportunities

1. **Agricultural residues** offer the largest untapped potential (5 billion tonnes/year)
2. **Industrial organic waste** has highest disposal costs creating strong economic drivers
3. **Food processing waste** presents seasonal but concentrated opportunities
4. **Developing regions** face disposal crises that WTE can address

### Critical Success Factors

1. **Policy support** for renewable energy and waste diversion
2. **Integrated waste collection** systems to ensure feedstock supply
3. **Appropriate technology selection** matched to local waste characteristics
4. **Public acceptance** through transparent communication
5. **Economies of scale** through regional hub development

### Data Gaps

1. Limited real-time data on waste composition in developing countries
2. Inconsistent reporting of industrial waste volumes
3. Seasonal variation data for agricultural residues
4. Actual disposal costs vs. reported costs
5. Local energy market pricing dynamics
